Examining How Exercise Can Help Young Adults with Depression: A Qualitative Study on Physical Activity and Mental Health

Introduction

One mental health issue that continues to plague people throughout the world is depression. Individuals with depression experience continuous sadness and loss of interest in engaging in activities. Other signs associated with depression include hopelessness and difficulty performing regular activities. This condition is experienced mainly by young adults who experience many challenges such as academic, social, and financial issues. Physical activities such as exercise are those that have been planned and are intended to ensure that individuals become physically healthy. Recent findings show that exercise is not only useful in helping people become physically fit but can also assist in improving their mental health status. Exercise has shown potential for enhancing psychological well-being, cognition, and emotional regulation (aan het Rot et al., 2009; Rodin, 1989). This study seeks to explain how exercise could help young adults suffering from depression through a comparison of biological and psychosocial approaches.

Scope and Definitions

Depression is defined as a mental disorder that manifests itself as prolonged periods of sadness, anhedonia, tiredness, and problems in functioning. Physical activity encompasses all forms of activities that require energy use, whereas exercise is a form of physical activity that requires deliberate effort. The term young adulthood is used to describe persons within the age range of 18 to 30 years. In this study, emphasis is placed on understanding the impact of exercise on depression in young people and students.

Biological Explanation

The biological theory claims that exercise reduces depression due to changes in physiology in the body and brain. Scientific research has demonstrated that exercise leads to the increase of neurotransmitters responsible for positive moods like serotonin and norepinephrine and encourages brain neurogenesis.

According to Smith et al., participants who participated in an eight-week aerobics program had increased amounts of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF) and decreased depressive symptoms. The researchers hypothesized that neurogenesis could be one of the mechanisms that led to mood improvement. In addition, Ma claimed that exercise enhances neurotransmitter activity, produces BDNF, and improves psychological well-being and cognitive processes. As Ma stated, exercise leads to an increase of such neurotransmitters as serotonin, norepinephrine, acetylcholine, and GABA.
Further proof can be found in research conducted by Gondoh et al. (2009) where the effects of aerobic exercise on university students were studied. It was found that exercise could maintain gray matter volumes in the brain's insular cortex, which plays an essential role in regulating emotions. Additionally, participants of the study who engaged in exercise reported much improved psychological well-being when compared to the control group. 

Further support can be found in the research conducted by Johnson and Lee (2020). The researchers demonstrated that exercise could enhance sleep quality, and sleep quality, in turn, could be related to the decrease of depression symptoms. Sleep might help to regulate mood, reduce fatigue and promote psychological well-being. Likewise, aaan het Rot et al. (2009) highlighted that a negative correlation existed between exercise and depression. Physical activity could result in less depression among people.
One of the most powerful advantages of the biological perspective is that it utilizes objective markers such as BDNF levels, neurotransmitter actions, sleep efficiency, and brain scans to show the physiological impact of exercise on mental well-being. Such physiological markers demonstrate that there is strong empirical support for the notion that exercise does have a significant biological effect which results in improved mental well-being. Nonetheless, one possible shortcoming of the biological perspective lies in the potential failure to explain differences in people’s outcomes from exercise.

Psychosocial Explanation

The psychosocial perspective considers the social and psychological effects of exercising. Instead of looking at physiological changes, this approach implies that social support, self-efficacy, behavioral activation, and positive mood all play roles in diminishing depressive symptoms.

As found by Brown et al. (2019), students who engaged in group physical exercises had reduced depressive symptoms compared to those who exercised alone. This means that social support can be one of the factors connecting exercise with mental health improvements. Furthermore, Patel and Wong (2022) noted that peer support promoted the involvement of individuals in physical activities and enhanced their wellbeing.
Furthermore, Zhang et al. (2023) showed that social support has a direct effect on the involvement in exercises and an indirect effect on physical activity mediated by self-efficacy. In their study involving 1,440 university students, peer, family, and school social support were shown to contribute to exercise behavior with the latter being the most influential.

Another body of research focused on university students indicated that exercise interventions help relieve stress, anxiety, and depression as well as increase quality of life. It was revealed that low-to-moderate intensity aerobic exercise is particularly beneficial in achieving these effects. Likewise, Rodin (1989) asserted that exercise makes contributions to psychological well-being through stress relief and increases life satisfaction.
One significant advantage of psychosocial theories is their recognition of individual differences, and they acknowledge the significance of social interaction. Nonetheless, there may be challenges in measuring some psychosocial constructs, which differ depending on the population involved.

Critique of Evidence

While the literature supports the positive correlation between exercise and depression, several issues must be addressed. To begin with, most experiments have been carried out using a small number of participants. Smith et al. (2021) and Gondoh et al. (2009) used a small sample size of 30 participants.

Furthermore, most of the experiments conducted measure the physical activity and depression levels through self-report, thus leading to response bias. Additionally, cross-sectional studies cannot prove causality since they do not indicate whether exercises lead to lower rates of depression or whether people who are less depressed are motivated to work out (aan het Rot et al., 2009).

Other variables, which include sleep, use of medication, social relationships, academic pressure, and access to mental health care, among others, may affect the outcome of experiments and need to be controlled. In conclusion, most of the research uses college students as study subjects; hence, the applicability of results is questionable.

What Would Change My Mind?

However, my conclusions would be altered if further large studies revealed that there were no measurable improvements in depressive symptoms with respect to exercise. Furthermore, if psychological treatment strategies without exercise led to similar results, the role of exercise itself would need to be reevaluated. Proof that exercise did not have any biological effects on the regulation of mood would also refute the biological perspective.

Conclusion

Physical exercise seems to be a beneficial technique in alleviating the symptoms of depression in young adults. Biological theories focus on the creation of new cells, neurotransmission, brain plasticity, and better sleep, whereas psychosocial theories consider the influence of social support, self-efficacy, behavioral engagement, and psychological wellbeing. Despite the advantages and disadvantages of each theory, none can explain the research findings independently. According to the results discussed in this paper, the biopsychosocial theory best explains the effect of exercise on the mental state of depressed young people.
